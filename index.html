Shorebirds' Revival

Extreme weather may benefit shorebirds, but human activities hinder that.

By Chuqin Jiang

When Samantha Robinson saw JYJ from a mile away in a spotting scope, she knew he would turn away soon, as he always did. He was shy and evasive, avoiding human beings for a long time.

JYJ is a piping plover, named after the number on his banded flag. Since he got his band in 2015, he was never trapped again by the researchers.

Every plover is different.” Said Robinson, who started studying piping plovers and other shorebirds as a part of the Virginia Tech team in 2016.

“You have plovers that are gonna stick right next to their nest and let you know that they're upset with whatever you're doing right in your face, and there are other ones that all go fly away and hide and you'll never see them again.”

Piping plovers are among the two-thirds of bird species under the threat of climate change in North America. As a conservation-reliant species, which means their survival depends on active human management, piping plovers are in need of scientists’ monitoring, as well as public consciousness of protection. However, scientists recently found a certain amount of sea-level rise and hurricanes may benefit the shorebirds.

From 2013 to 2018, the team that Robinson was in captured and banded 152 adults and 353 chicks on the Fire and Westhampton barrier islands, New York. It’s a part of a series of studies on how piping plovers reacted to the change brought by Hurricane Sandy nine years ago. As the strong wind removed vegetation and expanded sandy area, new habitats suitable for piping plovers are created. But scientists also pointed out that hard facilities are hindering barrier islands from natural movement.

Piping plovers are brownish-grey shore birds with a round body, big black eyes, and a short, sharp orange black-tipped bill. They usually have shallow scrape nests in the softy sandy area with sparse vegetation along the shores. In late March, they will fly through the Atlantic Flyway to breed on the beaches of New York and Connecticut.

In the late 18th century, plovers were the popular target of hunters, as their feathers can be decorated on hats and clothes. With the Migratory Bird Treaty Act of 1918, they were under the protection and their population increased until 1950. Due to the habitats loss, as more beaches are developed for recreation, the number began to decline again. In 1985, the Atlantic Coast and Northern Great Plains populations were listed as threatened, and the population in the Great Lakes watershed was listed as endangered, according to US Fish & Wildlife service.

The 2021 Atlantic Coast piping plover population estimate of 2,289 pairs is 22 percent higher than the 2018 estimate, and almost triple the estimate of 790 pairs at the time of the 1986 ESA listing.

The largest and most sustained population increase has occurred in New England. The number has reached its recovery goal, 625 pairs, for almost 20 years.
